How to Reduce Stress

Practical, evidence-informed habits you can try today — aligned with what Mood Manager’s predictor found most strongly linked with stress.

Pending tasks (strongest) Reduce interruptions (moderate) Improve sleep (moderate)
Note: Mood Manager is a hackathon demo. This page is general information, not medical or professional advice.

1) Shrink the “open loops” (pending tasks)

High impact

A big backlog of unfinished tasks can keep your brain “working” after work through rumination and persistent work-related thoughts. Research on unfinished tasks (often discussed via the Zeigarnik effect / rumination frameworks) links unfinished work to poorer recovery and sleep.

Try this today (10 minutes)

  1. Brain dump everything on your mind.
  2. Pick the top 3 that matter most.
  3. Convert each into a next action (tiny, specific).
  4. Schedule one action for tomorrow.

Make it stick

  • Use a single list (not multiple apps)
  • Limit “in progress” tasks (WIP) to 1–3
  • End-of-day shutdown ritual: review + plan first step
Evidence summary: unfinished work tasks are associated with more work-related rumination and impaired recovery/sleep in workplace research.

2) Reduce interruptions (protect focus)

Medium impact

Frequent interruptions and task switching can increase perceived workload and stress — especially when you’re working on something complex. Research in workplace and healthcare settings has found links between interruptive workflows and stress, and lab-style studies show physiological stress responses to multitasking/interruptions.

Quick wins

  • Turn off non-essential notifications
  • Use focus blocks (25–45 minutes)
  • Batch comms: check messages at set times
  • One-tab rule for deep work
  • Keep a “later” list for ideas that pop up
  • Use a status: “In focus until 2pm”
Evidence summary: frequent work interruptions are associated with higher workload/stress and can trigger stress-related physiological responses.

3) Improve sleep (stress buffer)

Moderate impact

Less sleep (and poorer sleep quality) is consistently associated with worse mental health outcomes and greater emotional reactivity. Even small improvements in sleep regularity can help your stress baseline.

Tonight

  • Set a realistic bedtime
  • Cool + dark room
  • Phone away from bed

This week

  • Keep wake time within ~1 hour
  • Morning light exposure
  • Caffeine earlier in the day

If you’re wired

  • Write tomorrow’s top 3 tasks
  • 2–5 minutes slow breathing
  • Short wind-down routine
Evidence summary: meta-analyses link sleep duration/quality with mental health outcomes; improving sleep supports emotional regulation and stress resilience.

4) Use EFT (“Tapping”) to downshift stress quickly

Optional

EFT (“tapping”) is a brief self-regulation technique that pairs focused attention on a stressor with tapping on acupressure points. Some research suggests EFT may reduce stress and anxiety symptoms and improve calmness in the short term, although study quality and findings can vary.

A simple 60–90 second “tapping break”

  1. Name the stress in one sentence (“I’m stressed about ___”).
  2. Rate it (0–10).
  3. Tap each point while repeating a short phrase (“this stress”, “this pressure”).
  4. Take one slow breath and re-rate.

Great between meetings or after an interruption spiral.

Pair it with the model’s top drivers

  • Pending tasks: tap for 60 seconds, then choose one “next action”
  • Interruptions: tap, then start a 25-minute focus block
  • Low sleep: tap as part of a wind-down routine

Safety note

  • If tapping brings up strong emotions, pause and switch to grounding (walk, breathing)
  • For ongoing distress, seek professional support
Evidence note: reviews/meta-analyses have reported reductions in anxiety/stress symptoms with EFT in some studies, though evidence quality varies. Examples: PubMed (EFT review) · PMC (EFT overview)
