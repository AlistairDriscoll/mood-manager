How to Improve Your Mood

Practical, evidence-informed habits you can try today — based on what Mood Manager’s predictor found most strongly linked with mood.

Exercise (strongest) Reduce screen time (moderate) Sleep a bit more (smaller boost)
Note: Mood Manager is a hackathon demo. This page is for general information, not medical advice.

1) Move your body (even a little)

High impact

Across many studies, exercise is consistently associated with improved mood and reduced depressive symptoms. The best “type” is often the one you’ll actually keep doing — walking/jogging, strength training, and yoga show strong results in large evidence reviews.

Try this today

  • 10–20 min walk outdoors (easy win)
  • 2–3 sets of bodyweight moves (squats, pushups, rows)
  • Short yoga flow (5–15 minutes)

Make it stick

  • Lower the bar: “show up” beats “go hard”
  • Attach it to a trigger: after lunch / after work
  • Track streaks: small consistency > intensity
Evidence: large systematic reviews/meta-analyses support exercise as an effective intervention for depressive symptoms. See references below.

2) Reduce screen time (especially “doom scroll”)

Medium impact

Higher screen time (and some types of social media use) is linked with worse mood and higher risk of depressive symptoms in multiple cohort studies and meta-analyses. This doesn’t mean “screens are evil” — it means your dose and type matter.

Quick swaps that work

  • Set a 30–60 min “no phone” block after waking
  • Move social apps off your home screen
  • Turn off non-essential notifications
  • Replace night scrolling with a short routine: shower / stretch / tea
  • Use “one screen at a time” (no TV + phone combo)
  • Try a screen curfew 30 min before bed
Evidence: prospective cohort meta-analyses and large observational studies report associations between higher screen time/social media use and depressive symptoms. See references below.

3) Sleep a bit more (and more consistently)

Smaller boost

In Mood Manager’s model, sleep had a smaller direct link to mood than exercise — but good sleep still supports emotional regulation and resilience. Sleep deprivation and disruption are repeatedly associated with worse emotional outcomes across research.

Tonight

  • Set a realistic bedtime
  • Cool, dark room
  • Charge phone away from bed

This week

  • Keep wake time within ~1 hour
  • Morning light exposure
  • Caffeine earlier in the day

If you’re restless

  • Short wind-down routine
  • Write tasks down (offload brain)
  • Gentle stretching / breathing
Evidence: systematic reviews link sleep disruption/deprivation with poorer emotional outcomes. See references below.
